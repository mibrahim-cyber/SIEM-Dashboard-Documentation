BREACH 001 · 2019

The Supply Chain Night

On a quiet Tuesday in December, Meridian-7's nightly ingest pipeline processed update package SW-ORION-2019.4.2 exactly as it had for three prior quarters. The binary carried a valid Authenticode signature from a vendor we had whitelisted since 2017. Hash reputation services returned clean. The file landed in the trusted publisher lane, bypassing sandbox detonation entirely. Cobalt Strike beacons began phoning home forty-one hours later — not from workstations, but from the build server that compiled our internal tooling.

Our SIEM saw millions of benign DNS queries and routine HTTPS to CDN edges. Nothing tripped severity thresholds because the outbound sessions mirrored legitimate update traffic: same User-Agent family, same TLS fingerprint, same geographic endpoints we had classified as software distribution. The attackers understood our correlation model better than our junior analysts did. They staged payloads inside signed DLLs that only activated after a dormant window, evading our YARA sweeps on day zero.

When EDR finally flagged an anomalous reflective DLL load on srv-build-03, the incident was already in its second week. Crown-jewel credentials had transited the same pipe we used for ticket attachments. Forensics recovered SUNBURST-style callbacks masked as Orion telemetry. The seventy-two-hour blind spot was not a tooling failure — it was a policy failure. We trusted the publisher more than we verified the artifact.

The memorial lesson is blunt: trust without telemetry is just faith with a longer mean time to detect. Supply chain compromise rewrote our detection philosophy. Every signed binary now earns a behavioral baseline, not a free pass.

"The hash was valid. The signature was valid. The trust was fatal." — Lead IR Analyst, Night Shift 2019

BREACH 002 · 2020

The Ransomware Pivot

It started on ws-finance-17 with a single macro-enabled spreadsheet delivered through a thread hijack on a vendor invoice chain. The user clicked Enable Content at 09:14 UTC. Within eleven minutes, a PowerShell cradle downloaded a staged loader from a compromised WordPress host. Our endpoint agent logged the execution but classified it as LOW because the parent process was WINWORD.EXE — a pattern we had tuned down after a flood of false positives in Q3.

By 13:40, the attacker pivoted to backup-srv-02 using stolen service credentials harvested from LSASS. Ransomware deployment began at 14:02 while our SOC drowned in noise: four hundred decoy alerts from a parallel port-scan campaign flooded the queue, burying the real lateral movement events. Analysts spent forty minutes clearing junk before anyone noticed backup volumes mounting with suspicious VSS deletion commands.

The real exfiltration ran over DNS tunneling to 198.51.100.44, encoding 2.1 GB of finance archives in TXT queries that looked like telemetry heartbeats. Our DNS anomaly rule existed but was set to MED severity with a twelve-hour aggregation window — useless against a four-hour smash-and-grab. Encryption hit domain controllers at 15:18. Recovery took nine days.

We learned that alert volume is an adversary's cheapest weapon. Decoy floods are not distraction — they are denial of analyst attention. Ransomware is rarely the first move; it is the loudest move after quiet staging.

"Four hundred false alarms bought them four hours. That was the real ransom." — SOC Shift Lead, October 2020

BREACH 003 · 2021

The Cloud Misconfiguration

A Terraform apply on March 3 introduced a single character error in an S3 bucket policy template: PublicReadAccess where PrivateReadAccess was intended. The bucket meridian-cust-records-prod held 4.7 million customer metadata rows. CloudTrail logged the policy change within seconds. Our SIEM ingested the event. Nothing fired for eleven days.

The rule s3-public-exposure had been authored during our on-premises migration and scoped exclusively to agents tagged zone=on-prem. Cloud control plane logs routed to a separate index with no correlation to the rule pack. External scanners discovered the bucket on day four. A security researcher emailed our abuse alias on day nine. We confirmed unauthorized enumeration on day eleven when GuardDuty-equivalent telemetry finally crossed into the unified index after a manual pipeline fix.

Attackers did not exfiltrate the full corpus — they sampled records to validate resale value, then listed access on a broker forum. The breach was configuration, not exploitation. No malware, no phishing, no stolen credentials. Just a typo and an index boundary that made invisible what should have screamed.

Cloud misconfiguration taught us that hybrid SIEM without unified scope is half-blind. A rule that only watches half your estate protects none of it when the other half holds the crown jewels.

"The bucket was public for eleven days. The rule was blind for eleven years of on-prem thinking." — Cloud Security Architect, Post-Mortem 2021

BREACH 004 · 2022

The Insider Exfiltration

Senior data engineer K.R. had held privileged access for four years. Credentials were MFA-protected. VPN sessions originated from approved residential IP ranges during normal working hours. UEBA scored the account at 12 — well inside green. Nothing about the behavior looked wrong because behavior was never the signal. Volume was.

Over nineteen nights, K.R. archived research datasets to an encrypted zip staging folder on a personal cloud sync directory mapped through a approved vendor tool. Each transfer stayed under the per-session threshold that triggered DLP review. The aggregate reached 890 GB before a storage quota alert from the sync vendor — not our SIEM — raised the first flag. Our data-volume rule existed but averaged over thirty-day windows, smoothing spikes into noise.

Investigation revealed departure plans submitted to HR six weeks after exfil began. The anomaly was not login time or geo-velocity — it was entropy: classified model weights leaving a segment that had never exported bulk data before. Baselines built on averages miss intentional slow leaks. Context — role, project lifecycle, departure timeline — was absent from our correlation engine.

Insider threat detection without baselines per data class is security theater. Trust is necessary; blind trust is negligent. We now baseline egress by asset criticality, not just by user history.

"The credentials were legitimate. The hours were normal. The volume was the confession." — Insider Threat Program Manager, 2022

BREACH 005 · 2023

The API Key Harvest

Meridian-7 rotated API keys quarterly across three documented stores: HashiCorp Vault, GitHub Actions secrets, and the internal config service. A fourth location existed only in a deprecated Lambda environment variable set — missed during the 2022 migration audit. Attackers found it through a public gist containing a redacted stack trace that still leaked the env prefix MERIDIAN_PROD_.

With the stale key, they queried customer lookup endpoints at 120 requests per minute — just under the per-key rate limit of 150. Each response returned PII slices used to enrich a credential stuffing list sold downstream. Our rate-limit rule fired at request 151 on minute forty-seven of the campaign. Correlation to threat intel occurred twelve hours later when a partner bank shared matching fraud signatures.

Twelve hours is an eternity at API speed. Automated abuse scales horizontally; human triage scales linearly. The key was revoked at T+14h. Scope damage: 38,000 customer records touched, 2,100 confirmed fraudulent login attempts externally. No shell access, no malware — pure credential abstraction at machine velocity.

Key rotation without inventory is rotation theater. Rate limits are detection triggers, not prevention. We now map every secret to an owner, a TTL, and a SIEM heartbeat that screams when an unknown key speaks.

"We rotated keys in four places. The attackers found the fifth. Rate limits told us twelve hours too late." — API Security Lead, Q2 2023 Review

BREACH 006 · 2024

The Zero-Day

On a Tuesday patch cycle, CVE-2024-XXXX hit our edge VPN concentrator before vendor advisories reached our threat feed. Exploitation required no authentication — only a malformed UDP handshake that crashed the IPS inspection path and opened a memory-corruption primitive. The first sign was not a signature match. It was lateral movement at machine speed: four hosts compromised in ninety seconds with identical parent-child process trees our EDR had never cataloged.

Zero IOCs existed for forty-eight hours. YARA returned nothing. Threat intel feeds showed green across the board. What saved us was not detection — it was response automation written after the 2019 supply chain memorial: isolation playbooks triggered on anomalous lateral velocity alone, severing segments when three or more hosts exhibited identical rare process spawn chains within a five-minute window.

Three segments isolated automatically at T+7m. Two were false positives on a legitimate deployment tool — acceptable collateral. One segment contained the live attacker pivot path. Manual IR joined at T+22m. Full containment at T+4h. No data exfil confirmed. The zero-day was patched at T+36h when the vendor dropped firmware.

Zero-day Tuesday proved that playbooks outlive signatures. When you cannot recognize the weapon, you must recognize the motion. Machine-speed lateral movement is its own IOC — if your automation is brave enough to act on pattern alone.

"No signature. No IOC. Only velocity — and the playbooks we wrote when trust finally broke." — Meridian-7 Memorial Archive · 2024
